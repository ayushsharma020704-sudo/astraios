The Milky Way Galaxy: Structure and Formation

The Milky Way is a barred spiral galaxy with an estimated diameter of 100,000 to 180,000 light-years. It contains between 100 billion and 400 billion stars and at least that many planets. Our Solar System is located about 26,000 light-years from the Galactic Center, within one of the galaxy's spiral arms known as the Orion Arm (or Orion Spur).

The Milky Way is estimated to have formed approximately 13.6 billion years ago, making it nearly as old as the universe itself. The galaxy formed through a process of hierarchical merging — smaller proto-galactic fragments collided and merged over billions of years. The oldest known stars in the Milky Way, found in globular clusters, are around 12 to 13 billion years old.

The galaxy consists of several distinct structural components. The thin disk, where most stars and gas reside, has a thickness of about 1,000 light-years. The thick disk extends to about 3,000 light-years and contains older, metal-poor stars. Surrounding the disk is the stellar halo, a roughly spherical distribution of old stars and globular clusters extending far beyond the visible disk.

At the very center of the Milky Way lies Sagittarius A* (Sgr A*), a supermassive black hole with a mass of approximately 4 million times that of our Sun. The Event Horizon Telescope collaboration released the first image of Sgr A* in May 2022, confirming its nature as a black hole.

The Milky Way is part of the Local Group, a collection of more than 80 galaxies. Its nearest large galactic neighbor is the Andromeda Galaxy (M31), located about 2.5 million light-years away. The Milky Way and Andromeda are on a collision course and are expected to merge in about 4.5 billion years, forming a new elliptical galaxy sometimes called "Milkomeda."

The galaxy rotates differentially, with stars closer to the center orbiting faster than those farther out. The Sun takes approximately 225 to 250 million years to complete one orbit around the Galactic Center — a period sometimes called a "cosmic year" or "galactic year."
